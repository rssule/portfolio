## Overview
### Ending Childhood Food Insecurity in New Mexico
<p>The report highlights the unfortunate issue of food insecurity in New Mexico that has been worsened by COVID-19; limiting access to healthy and nutritious food for many children within the state. The report also discusses current evidence-based programs aimed at addressing food insecurity and its root causes. It is however pointed out that there are oftentimes barriers to accessing the programs or limited resource allocation from the programs. The report proposes several policy recommendations in line with a holistic approach to addressing both financial and food insecurity in a bid to ensure reliable access to healthy food for New Mexico families during and after the pandemic. In describing the food insecurity issue, the report uses a basic choropleth to show the varying food insecurity rates in 2020 across New Mexico counties and highlights the percentage point increase from 2019.</p>

## Critique
### Usefulness
  <p>For the intended audience, the figure communicates valuable information which makes it useful – but I would argue that it is a bit overwhelming since the choropleth uses color to highlight counties with higher food insecurity rates but also includes the rates as labels along with the increase from 2019. I rate it an 8 for usefulness.</p>
  
### Completeness
  <p>Overall, the graph has the necessary information to make it understandable. The legend indicates the key for understanding the use of color in the figure. The title and subtitle communicate that the percentage labels within the figure represent child food insecurity rates in 2020 across the state’s counties. It is overall a generally complete graph in terms of information so I rate it 9 for completeness.</p>
  
### Perceptibility
  <p>I do not believe the reader can understand the information in the figure with minimal effort because the percentage labels being included in the figure make it a bit overwhelming and busy, leading to a lot of eye travel. The visualization type is not entirely inappropriate, if the percentage labels were not included and it was just the choropleth with the legend, it would have been perceivable with minimal effort – with a perception that the figure is showing child food insecurity rates across counties within New Mexico. Consequently, with an understanding that spatial charts are used when precise geographic location matters, the visualization type is not entirely bad. But I think the report tried to highlight the high rates across the state and also call out Luna’s high rate; however, this is not exactly evident from the figure. I rate this a 7 for perceptibility.</p>
  
### Truthfulness
  <p>I think the visualization is accurate, reliable and valid because it does not seem to be misrepresenting the information. It seems it is representing what it says it is in a truthful manner i.e. child food insecurity rates in 2020 by counties within New Mexico. It is debatable the validity of the rates used but the report cites its sources as reports from Feeding America (which is a reputable source). The figure doesn’t seem to make incorrect comparisons; however, the figure refers to 2019 rates and notes percentage point changes. The 2019 rates are not in the figure or report so it is unverified whether these percentage point changes are valid or not. I rate this a 9 for truthfulness.</p>
  
### Intuitiveness
  <p>The figure is not entirely difficult to understand but it does take some effort to understand what the visualization trying to show because it is quite busy with text. It includes a key for the colors, which is helpful. But the text-heavy labelling makes the intuitiveness shaky because on one hand, you’d try to use the key to understand the figure but then on the other hand, you’d see all these in-figure labelling. Based on this, I rate this a 7 for intuitiveness.</p>
  
### Aesthetics
  <p>In my opinion, it is pretty interesting figure but again, the text-heaviness makes it a bit unpleasant to look at. The use of color for the different counties to denote the rates is a good choice since a heavier hue is used for higher numbers and lighter hue for lower numbers. But the switching between black and white for the in-figure labelling does not seem like a good idea. The title, subtitles, legend and footer are all in black, which is good and not distracting or attention seeking. Even the title being bolded is pretty good, in my opinion, as it helps to start off the reader by drawing some attention but not in a distracting way. I would say it is a pretty average visualization overall, it is a bit pleasing but also quite distracting because of the business – but I acknowledge that worse could have certainly been done. I rate this an 8 for aesthetics.</p>
  
### Engagement
  <p>I think the topic itself is quite intriguing so a graph showing the variation of food insecurity rates across the counties does spark interest and could lead to the audience wanting to learn more about the issue. Consequently, it could most likely inspire the audience to talk about the data and share it with others. Food insecurity is a serious and imminent problem so rates as high as 38% are really problematic! I rate this a 9 for engagement.</p>
  
### Prompt 1: Overall Observations
  <p>My initial reaction to the figure was that it was it was a decent work but could use some improvement so the main message is communicated efficiently and clearly. I could immediately imagine how worse off the figure could have been so I did appreciate the good use of color and the inclusion of information. I did think the text was overly distracting but I could also understand why it seemed important to include the numbers because of the issue of food insecurity being addressed in the report.</p>
<p>I liked how the main title indicates explicitly that the rates vary widely by county but then it was not so evident in the figure to immediately notice that since I had to look around a bit to even figure out the range. I think it is helpful to have seen the geographic locations of the counties as presented in the choropleth but it seemed like there was information overload so that aspect did not work too well. I was also unsure why the information above the legend was included, I know what it is because I read the report but I do not know that it exactly does much (if anything) to support the figure.</p>
<p>Although I like the choropleth, I would actually change it to a bar graph. Here’s why: I read the report so I know that this figure is trying to communicate a few things. It should show the varying rates across the counties but it should also highlight that the rates go as high as 38. Based on this premise, I would use a bar graph, order the counties in descending order of rates, highlight the highest rate and also highlight the high rate upfront in the title. Unfortunately, I would drop the percentage point changes because I do not believe they are doing much to help the message here. Thus, subtitle, legend and sentences above the legend would also be dropped as they would not be needed. Lastly, I would minimize the color and use a deep orange to highlight the county with the highest rate then grey out the others. I think this will help achieve the two main points stated at the start of this paragraph.
</p>
  
  
### Prompt 2: Audience
  <p>The primary audience for this tool are New Mexico policymakers; those that can implement policies to reduce and eventually eradicate food insecurity in the state. I do not think this visualization is effective in reaching that audience because although it presents useful information, it does not have the right callouts to get the attention of busy policymakers. For this main audience, a clear and minimalist graph that highlights focus areas and shows size could be more effective.</p>
  
### Prompt 3: Final Thoughts
  <p>I think this critique method was quite effective in evaluating the selected data visualization. It helped me think through the different aspects of the figure and was useful in spotting trends that made the figure faulty. But it was also helpful in highlighting that sometimes the visualizations only need some tweaking to make them better. Lastly it helped to think about the appropriateness of the chosen visualization type for the primary audience; which is very key because it might be an aesthetically pleasing figure that does not meet the audience needs or communicate efficiently. I would say that I think I prefer this method to the Goods Charts method – which is similar but different.</p>
  <p>I think I would add a measure for “<i>data relationship</i>”, which would help evaluate whether the visualization is appropriate for the data relationship in the story e.g. thinking through whether ranking or distribution or change over time or correlation is what is most important. I would also consider color being a separate measure from aesthetics because I think it would be helpful to focus on analyzing the use of color in communicating and its effectiveness in the visualization or lack thereof.</p>

## Sketch/Wireframe
  From the critique, here are the key considerations for a redesign that informed the wireframing process:
-	Changing the figure to a bar graph to visually show variation of rates across counties
-	Changing the use of color to callout county with high rate
-	Dropping legend and other extraneous information in the graph e.g. percentage point changes from 2019 and the state rate for all ages and children, highlighting only a few rates
-	Updating the title to callout key message

## Sketch Feedback
  After developing the wireframe, feedback was sought from two individuals:

- Adult, late 50’s, healthcare worker
  - The sketch shows a graph that shows Luna county has the highest food insecurity rate in the state at 38%.
  - It was unclear why some counties had numbers shown but others did not so it was suggested that all bars be labelled with rates for the respective county. It was indicated that showing all the numbers would give a clear picture and would not be distracting. Seeing all the county names on the y-axis was also not deemed as overwhelming. Also, it was noted that the heading provides the information to know what the graph is showing and highlights a high rate upfront (Luna county).
  - It was assumed that the intended audience would be the general public.
  
- Adult, late 20’s, student
  - The sketch shows food insecurity rate by county in New Mexico, ranking from highest to lowest. This shows that Luna county has the highest food insecurity rate in the state.
  - It was unclear how the rate is derived and its basis so it was suggested to include the formula for calculating the rate and the year of the data shown.

<i>Similarities</i>
<p>It is clear that the graph shows child food insecurity rates for New Mexico counties and Luna county’s 38% stands out as the highest. This indicates that the callout being implement in the sketch has achieved its purpose!</p>

<i>Differences</i>
<p>The first interviewee was more focused on the communicativeness of the visualization – in other words how complete the story was; on the other hand, but sort of similarly, the second interviewee was focused on the validity of the rates and their mathematical formulation – which also focuses on how complete the story is.</p>

<i>Learnings</i>
<p>It was interesting to note that highlighting the highest rate through the use of color and changing the title worked in putting focus on the rate. Both interviewees did not seem distracted or overly confused by the sketch but both wanted some more information included in the graph. </p>
<p>My response would be to include the year in the title and label all the counties & rates. I decided to not include extra information on how the rates were calculated because that is not information that needs to be on the graph for the intended primary audience and also, that information could be distracting if included as it is more suited in the body or appendix of the report.</p>
